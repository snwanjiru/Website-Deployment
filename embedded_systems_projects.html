DESIGN AND TESTING OF A CAPACITIVE SENSOR CIRCUIT FOR FUEL ADULTERATION DETECTION (PoC)

Use of the Miller Effect—an analogue circuit technique—to electronically amplify a small capacitance of ~20pF into a larger, more easily measurable effective capacitance (~1nF).

The Goal of this PoC: To validate that a Miller effect circuit could successfully amplify a small capacitance, proving the core concept for a future miniaturized, portable sensor.

Following a deep dive into various component datasheets and theory, the above setup was achieved with the need to design a stable, high-gain, high-impedance system. I excited the circuit with a 1MHz square wave and used an oscilloscope to probe critical nodes: the op-amp output, the virtual ground, and the final buffered output, measuring the RC time constant to calculate the effective capacitance.

The challenge was balancing the op-amp's stability, gain, and the extreme sensitivity to parasitic capacitances / elements.

The core principle of the Miller Effect—that a feedback capacitor appears multiplied by the op-amp's gain—was validated in its ability to profoundly influence the circuit's behavior. The circuit did not behave neutrally; it reacted strongly, proving the effect's presence.

However, the results conclusively showed that in a high-impedance, high-frequency configuration, the theoretical amplification is easily dominated by parasitic capacitance and op-amp instability.

In essence, this project successfully de-risked the development path. It proved that the challenge is no longer a question of circuit theory, but one of precision implementation
